## 8.1 Why 671 billion parameters won’t fit in your pocket

We have built something remarkable over the course of this book. The DeepSeek-R1 model, with its Multi-Head Latent Attention, Mixture-of-Experts architecture, and reinforcement-learning-trained reasoning capabilities, is a state-of-the-art language model. But building a powerful model is only half the battle. The other half is deploying it, getting it into the hands of users who need it.

And this is where we hit a wall.

### 8.1.1 The cost of intelligence

Let’s put some concrete numbers on the problem. The full DeepSeek-R1 model has 671 billion total parameters. As we learned when we built the Mixture-of-Experts architecture in chapter 4, the MoE design means that only 37 billion parameters are *activated* per token, but the full model weights must still reside in memory. Every one of those 671 billion parameters needs to be stored and accessible, even if only a fraction are used for any given token.

In half-precision (FP16), storing 671 billion parameters requires approximately 1342 GB of GPU memory. For context, NVIDIA’s most powerful consumer GPU, the RTX 4090, has 24 GB of memory. The professional-grade H100 has 80 GB. Even a single H100 cannot hold this model. Running DeepSeek-R1 requires a minimum of two nodes of eight NVIDIA H100 GPUs working in concert. A configuration that costs upward of $200,000 in hardware and consumes enough electricity to power a small apartment building.

And the cost does not stop at hardware. During inference, every token the model generates requires a full forward pass through the active parameters. At 37 billion active parameters per token, and with the KV Cache demanding additional memory for long context windows (as we saw in chapter 2), the cost of generating a single response can range from cents to dollars, depending on the length. For a company serving millions of users, this adds up to thousands of dollars per day.

As illustrated in figure 8.2, there is a massive gap between the hardware required to run the full teacher model and the deployment targets where reasoning is actually needed.

![Figure 8.2 The deployment gap between DeepSeek-R1 (requiring a server cluster) and real-world targets where reasoning capabilities are needed, from data center GPUs down to mobile devices.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image002.png)
*Figure 8.2 The deployment gap between DeepSeek-R1 (requiring a server cluster) and real-world targets where reasoning capabilities are needed, from data center GPUs down to mobile devices.*

This is not just a theoretical concern. Consider the practical scenarios where a reasoning model would be most valuable:

* A student using a math tutoring app on their laptop needs step-by-step problem solving -but cannot afford a cloud API call for every question.
* A developer running a coding assistant locally needs it to fit on a single RTX 4090 -not a server rack.
* An offline medical diagnostic tool in a rural clinic needs to run on whatever hardware is available.

In all of these cases, the full 671B model is simply unusable. The intelligence is there, locked inside 671 billion parameters, but it is inaccessible to anyone who does not have a data center.

This creates a troubling imbalance. The organizations that can afford to deploy 671B models, large tech companies with massive GPU clusters, get the benefit of state-of-the-art reasoning. Everyone else is left behind. If we want AI reasoning to have a broad impact, we need to find a way to make it run on hardware that ordinary people and organizations actually have.

This is not just a nice-to-have. It is arguably one of the most important problems in modern AI: how do we bridge the gap between what the most powerful models can do and what can actually be deployed in the real world? The field has explored several approaches: quantization (which we covered in chapter 5) reduces precision to shrink memory footprint, pruning removes less important weights, and architecture design (like the MoE approach in chapter 4) increases efficiency. But the most powerful approach. The one that has produced the most compression ratios while preserving the most capability, is knowledge distillation.

### 8.1.2 The dream: reasoning in your pocket

But here is where the story gets interesting. When DeepSeek released the R1 paper in January 2025, they did not just release the massive 671B model. They also released six smaller, distilled models that compress the teacher’s reasoning capabilities into dramatically more compact forms. Figure 8.3 shows just how dramatic this compression is.

![Figure 8.3 The dramatic reduction in memory and GPU requirements from the 671B teacher model down to the smallest 1.5B distilled model, which runs on a phone.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image003.png)
*Figure 8.3 The dramatic reduction in memory and GPU requirements from the 671B teacher model down to the smallest 1.5B distilled model, which runs on a phone.*

The smallest of these, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, has just 1.5 billion parameters, roughly 0.2% of the teacher’s size. It requires only about 3 GB of memory and can run on a CPU or a mobile device. And yet, this tiny model achieves 28.9% on the AIME 2024 math competition benchmark, compared to GPT-4o’s 9.3%.

How is this possible? How can we compress a 671B-parameter model into one that is 450 times smaller and still preserve, or even exceed, the reasoning capabilities of frontier models?

You might think this sounds too good to be true, and there is indeed a catch. The 1.5B model does not beat GPT-4o at *everything*. It excels at mathematical reasoning (which compresses well into small models) but struggles with coding tasks (which require more capacity). We will explore these trade-offs in detail in section 8.8. But the headline result is genuine and deeply significant: reasoning capabilities can be dramatically compressed through the right training strategy.

The answer is **knowledge distillation**, and understanding it is the goal of this entire chapter. We will build the technique from first principles, starting with the intuition of why it works, developing the mathematics of temperature scaling, implementing it in PyTorch, and finally examining DeepSeek’s specific approach that produced these extraordinary results.

A note on the names. Each of these distilled models keeps the architecture and pretrained weights of its base model Qwen2.5 or Llama-3 completely intact. What DeepSeek-R1 contributes is training data: 800K curated reasoning examples (which we will examine in section 8.5) that are used to fine-tune the existing open-source bases. So "Distill-Qwen-1.5B" is literally Qwen2.5-Math-1.5B fine-tuned on R1-generated data. The compression in figure 8.3 is therefore not a transformation of R1's parameters; it is the transfer of R1's reasoning behavior into a different, smaller model that already existed.

## 8.2 The teacher-student paradigm

The idea behind knowledge distillation is deceptively simple. Instead of training a small model from scratch on raw data, we train it to mimic a larger, more capable model. The large model becomes the **teacher**, and the small model becomes the **student**. The teacher has already learned rich representations of the world. It knows not just the right answers, but *why* those answers are right, what alternatives are close, and how different concepts relate to each other. The goal of distillation is to transfer this rich understanding to the student.

> **Definition**  
> What is knowledge distillation? Knowledge distillation is a model compression technique where a small "student" model is trained to reproduce the behavior of a larger "teacher" model, transferring the teacher’s learned knowledge into a more compact and deployable form.

As illustrated in figure 8.4, the basic paradigm works as follows: the teacher model, which has already been trained and is now frozen (its parameters never change), processes the same training data as the student. But instead of training the student on the ground-truth labels, we train it to match the teacher’s output, specifically, the teacher’s full probability distribution over all possible outputs. We call this distribution the teacher’s "soft targets" in contrast to the one-hot "hard labels" of standard training; we will unpack this direction in 8.2.1.

![Figure 8.4 The teacher-student paradigm in knowledge distillation. The frozen teacher shares its soft probability distributions with the student, transferring richer information than hard labels alone.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image004.png)
*Figure 8.4 The teacher-student paradigm in knowledge distillation. The frozen teacher shares its soft probability distributions with the student, transferring richer information than hard labels alone.*

### 8.2.1 Why soft labels carry more information than hard labels

To understand why this approach works so well, we need to appreciate a subtle difference between **hard labels** and **soft labels**.

A hard label is a one-hot vector. When we train a model to classify images and the label says "cat", the model receives a vector like [1, 0, 0, 0]. All the probability mass is on the correct class, zero everywhere else. This provides the model with approximately one bit of information per training sample: the answer is "cat" and nothing else. The model learns nothing about which incorrect classes are "almost right" or how different classes relate to each other.

But consider what the teacher’s output probability distribution tells us. When a well-trained teacher sees an image of a cat and produces the distribution [cat: 86%, dog: 12%, fox: 1.5%, bird: 0.5%], it is communicating something remarkably rich. It is saying: “This is almost certainly a cat. But I can see why someone might think it is a dog; both are furry, four-legged household pets. A fox is less likely but shares some visual features like the pointed face and small body. A bird, on the other hand, looks nothing like this.”

This full probability distribution carries not just the correct answer but an entire *web of relationships* between classes. Each training sample now provides the equivalent of 10 or more bits of information, rather than just one. The student receives a rich, structured teaching signal that dramatically accelerates learning.

Figure 8.5 shows this difference side-by-side.

![Figure 8.5 Hard labels provide approximately 1 bit of information per sample (correct or incorrect), while the teacher’s soft labels reveal rich inter-class similarity structure. The dark knowledge.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image005.png)
*Figure 8.5 Hard labels provide approximately 1 bit of information per sample (correct or incorrect), while the teacher’s soft labels reveal rich inter-class similarity structure. The dark knowledge.*

The information about which wrong answers are "almost right" is what Geoffrey Hinton, Oriol Vinyals, and Jeff Dean called **dark knowledge** in their seminal 2015 paper, “Distilling the Knowledge in a Neural Network” Paper Link: [https://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.02531](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1503.02531).

The term "dark knowledge" is a deliberate analogy to dark matter in physics. Just as dark matter makes up the majority of the universe’s mass but is invisible to direct observation, dark knowledge constitutes the majority of what a teacher model has learned but is invisible in hard labels. It is only by looking at the soft probability distribution. The full spectrum of the teacher’s output, not just the top prediction. That we can observe and transfer this hidden knowledge.

The concept traces back even further than Hinton’s 2015 paper. In 2006, Buciluǎ, Caruana, and Niculescu-Mizil first proposed compressing ensemble models into single networks using pseudo-labels. In 2014, Ba and Caruana showed that shallow networks could match deep networks when trained on soft targets. But it was Hinton’s introduction of temperature scaling that unlocked the full potential of knowledge transfer and gave the field its modern form.

The dark knowledge is invisible in the hard labels, but it is precisely the information that allows the student to learn faster and generalize better.

> **Definition**  
> What is dark knowledge? Dark knowledge refers to the information encoded in the relative probabilities of incorrect classes in a teacher model’s output distribution. It captures inter-class similarity relationships that are invisible in hard one-hot labels.

### 8.2.2 The master chef analogy

Let's make this more intuitive, consider an analogy. Imagine you want to learn to cook, and you have two options.

**Option A: A recipe book.** The book tells you, “Use basil in this dish.” It gives you the correct answer, but nothing else. You have no idea why basil works, what you could substitute, or how different herbs relate to each other.

**Option B: A master chef.** The chef tells you, “Basil is the best choice here, about 52% of the flavor profile you want. But oregano would get you about 27% of the way there, thyme about 14%, and rosemary only about 7%. Basil and oregano share similar aromatic compounds, which is why they’re somewhat interchangeable.”

Figure 8.6 illustrates this analogy.

![Figure 8.6 Learning from a recipe book (hard labels) gives you the correct answer but no context, while learning from a master chef (soft labels) reveals the relationships between alternatives. The dark knowledge that enables deeper understanding.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image006.png)
*Figure 8.6 Learning from a recipe book (hard labels) gives you the correct answer but no context, while learning from a master chef (soft labels) reveals the relationships between alternatives. The dark knowledge that enables deeper understanding.*

The master chef’s guidance is vastly more informative. Each "lesson" carries not just the right answer but a rich web of relationships. An apprentice trained this way develops deeper culinary intuition. They can improvise, substitute, and generalize to new dishes they have never seen before.

Knowledge distillation works the same way. The teacher’s probability distribution is the master chef’s guidance, and the hard label is the recipe book. By learning from the full distribution, the student acquires a much richer understanding of the problem than it could ever get from hard labels alone.

This richer understanding manifests in a concrete, measurable way: **generalization**. A student trained with soft targets consistently performs better on data it has never seen before, compared to the same student trained with hard labels. This is because the soft targets teach the student not just the answers but the *structure* of the problem space, which classes are similar, which are different, and how confidence distributes across the possibilities. This structural knowledge transfers to new examples, even ones from slightly different distributions than the training data.

The practical impact of this idea has been demonstrated repeatedly since 2015. One of the most famous examples is **DistilBERT** (2019), which compressed the BERT language model to 40% fewer parameters while retaining 97% of its performance on the GLUE benchmark and running 60% faster. DistilBERT combined soft-target distillation with intermediate layer matching (having the student mimic the teacher’s hidden states, not just its outputs) to achieve this remarkable compression. It became one of the most widely deployed NLP models in the world, proving that distillation could bring powerful language understanding to resource-constrained environments.

But all of these successes were for *classification* or *language understanding* tasks, where the teacher and student share a fixed set of output classes. The application of distillation to *reasoning*, transferring a model’s ability to solve multi-step problems through chain-of-thought, is a newer and more challenging frontier that DeepSeek-R1 pushed to new heights.

Before we get to DeepSeek’s specific approach, though, we need to understand the mathematical machinery that makes classical distillation work. And it all starts with one question: how do we extract the dark knowledge from the teacher? The probabilities in a standard softmax output are often extremely peaked, 86.5% on the correct class and near-zero everywhere else. The dark knowledge is there, but it is hidden in the tiny probabilities. The answer lies in a single trick: **temperature**.

## 8.3 Temperature and dark knowledge

The relative ranking of incorrect classes is the key ingredient that makes distillation work. But we also noted a problem: under a standard softmax (at temperature T=1), the teacher’s output distribution is often highly peaked. The correct class gets nearly all the probability mass, and the subtle relationships between incorrect classes are buried in near-zero values.

To unlock the dark knowledge, Hinton et al. (2015) introduced a simple idea: **temperature scaling**. Instead of using the standard softmax, we divide the model’s logits (raw output scores) by a temperature parameter T before applying the softmax function.

### 8.3.1 The temperature-scaled softmax

The standard softmax function converts a vector of logits z into a probability distribution:

$$ p_i = \frac{\exp(z_i)}{\sum_j \exp(z_j)} $$

The temperature-scaled softmax adds a single parameter T:

$$ q_i = \frac{\exp(z_i / T)}{\sum_j \exp(z_j / T)} $$

When T = 1, this reduces to the standard softmax. When T > 1, the distribution becomes **softer**. The probabilities spread out more evenly, revealing the relative magnitudes of the logits. When T → ∞, the distribution becomes perfectly uniform, losing all information.

> **Definition**  
> What is temperature in knowledge distillation? Temperature (T) is a hyperparameter that controls the "softness" of the probability distribution produced by the softmax function. As T increases, the distribution spreads from peaked toward uniform. There is a useful middle range (typically T = 2–5) where this softening exposes the teacher's inter-class similarity structure; past that range, the distribution becomes so uniform that the structure is washed out. The section 8.3.5 Goldilocks discussion makes this precise.

### 8.3.2 A numerical walkthrough: seeing the dark knowledge emerge

Let's make this concrete with a numerical example. Suppose our teacher model processes an image and produces the following logits for four classes:

$$ z = [5.0, 3.0, 1.0, -1.0] $$

These correspond to classes "cat", "dog", "fox", and "bird". Let's see what happens when we apply softmax at three different temperatures.

**At T=1 (standard softmax):**

We compute exp([5.0, 3.0, 1.0, -1.0]) = [148.41, 20.09, 2.72, 0.37], with a sum of 171.59. Dividing by the sum gives us:

$$ p = [0.865, 0.117, 0.016, 0.002] $$

The teacher is 86.5% confident in "cat". The dark knowledge? Almost invisible. The probabilities for "fox" (1.6%) and "bird" (0.2%) are so small that any meaningful relationships between these classes are lost in the noise.

As figure 8.7 shows, this peaked distribution looks almost identical to a hard label.

![Figure 8.7 Standard softmax at T=1 for logits [5.0, 3.0, 1.0, -1.0]. The distribution is heavily peaked at “cat” (86.5%), hiding the dark knowledge in near-zero probabilities.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image009.png)
*Figure 8.7 Standard softmax at T=1 for logits [5.0, 3.0, 1.0, -1.0]. The distribution is heavily peaked at “cat” (86.5%), hiding the dark knowledge in near-zero probabilities.*

**At T=3 (the sweet spot):**

Now we divide the logits by 3 before applying softmax. Let's trace every step of the computation:

`Step 1. Divide by T: z/3 = [5.0/3, 3.0/3, 1.0/3, -1.0/3] = [1.667, 1.000, 0.333, -0.333]`

`Step 2. Exponentiate: exp([1.667, 1.000, 0.333, -0.333]) = [5.30, 2.72, 1.40, 0.72]`

`Step 3. Sum: 5.30 + 2.72 + 1.40 + 0.72 = 10.14`

`Step 4. Normalize: [5.30/10.14, 2.72/10.14, 1.40/10.14, 0.72/10.14]`

`q = [0.523, 0.268, 0.138, 0.071]`

Now the dark knowledge is clearly visible! The teacher reveals that:
* "Dog" has a 26.8% probability -the teacher sees dogs as somewhat cat-like (both are furry, four-legged pets).
* "Fox" has a 13.8% probability -foxes share some visual features with cats (similar body shape, pointed ears).
* "Bird" has only 7.1% -birds are the least similar to cats in the teacher's learned representations.

**At T=10 (too soft):**

At T=10, the distribution flattens to [0.329, 0.270, 0.221, 0.181], nearly uniform. The signal is almost completely lost. We have revealed so much "dark knowledge" that we have diluted the actual knowledge.

Figure 8.8 shows all three distributions side by side, making the effect of temperature immediately clear.

![Figure 8.8 The same logits [5.0, 3.0, 1.0, -1.0] processed through softmax at three temperatures. At T=1, the distribution is too peaked, at T=3 the dark knowledge emerges clearly, and at T=10 the signal is lost in near-uniformity.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image010.png)
*Figure 8.8 The same logits [5.0, 3.0, 1.0, -1.0] processed through softmax at three temperatures. At T=1, the distribution is too peaked, at T=3 the dark knowledge emerges clearly, and at T=10 the signal is lost in near-uniformity.*

The progression from left to right tells the entire story of temperature: too cold hides the dark knowledge, just right reveals it, too hot destroys the signal.

Let's quantify this more precisely. At T=1, the entropy of the distribution (a measure of how spread out it is) is quite low, about 0.59 bits. Most of the information is concentrated in the top class. At T=3, the entropy rises to 1.71 bits, nearly three times as much information per sample. At T=10, the entropy reaches 1.94 bits, approaching the theoretical maximum of 2 bits for a 4-class distribution. But maximum entropy means uniform distribution, which carries no useful information at all.

The key insight is that we do not want to maximize entropy. We want to maximize *useful* entropy. At T=3, the distribution is spread enough to reveal the ranking of classes (dog > fox > bird) without being so spread that the ranking becomes ambiguous. This is the mathematical basis for the Goldilocks zone.

There is another way to think about this. At T=1, a training sample says: "This is a cat. Full stop." At T=3, a training sample says: "This is probably a cat (52%), but it could be a dog (27%) or a fox (14%). It is definitely not a bird (7%)." At T=10, a training sample says: "I am not really sure what this is, could be any of the four classes with roughly equal probability." The middle message is by far the most informative.

### 8.3.3 What the dark knowledge reveals

Let's take a closer look at the T=3 distribution, because what it reveals is genuinely remarkable. Figure 8.9 illustrates the inter-class similarity structure that the teacher has learned.

![Figure 8.9 At T=3, the teacher’s soft targets reveal the inter-class similarity structure it has learned. Cats are somewhat dog-like (26.8%), slightly fox-like (13.8%), and very unlike birds (7.1%). This similarity structure IS the dark knowledge.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image011.png)
*Figure 8.9 At T=3, the teacher’s soft targets reveal the inter-class similarity structure it has learned. Cats are somewhat dog-like (26.8%), slightly fox-like (13.8%), and very unlike birds (7.1%). This similarity structure IS the dark knowledge.*

The teacher has implicitly learned a similarity hierarchy: cat -> dog -> fox -> bird, ordered by decreasing visual and semantic similarity to "cat". This is information that would take the student many more training examples to discover from hard labels alone. With soft targets, the student receives this rich structural information in every single training example, not just "the answer is cat", but "the answer is cat, and here is exactly how every other class relates to it".

With hard labels, the student needs to see many examples of cats being confused with dogs, and many examples of cats never being confused with birds, to gradually build up a statistical picture of inter-class similarity. Each training example provides a single bit of direction. With soft targets, every single example provides the complete similarity map. All four probabilities at once. The learning signal is not just stronger; it is qualitatively different. The student learns the structure of the class space, not just the location of each class within it.

This is why distilled students often outperform students trained from scratch, even when given fewer training examples. The richer supervision signal compensates for having less data. In some experiments, a distilled student trained on 10% of the data can match a scratch-trained student that uses the full dataset, because each soft-target example carries 10x more information than a hard-label example.

### 8.3.4 Why T² scaling matters

Before we move on, let's address a mathematical detail: the T² scaling factor.

When we divide logits by T before applying softmax, the gradient of the KL loss with respect to a student logit drops by two factors of 1/T, not one. Differentiating KL(p_T || q_T) with respect to a student logit z_s, where q_T = softmax(z_s / T), gives:

$$ \frac{\partial L_{soft}}{\partial z_s} \propto \frac{1}{T} (q_T - p_T) $$

The first 1/T comes from the chain rule (the / T inside the softmax). The second comes from the fact that the difference (q_T − p_T) itself shrinks as T grows, both distributions flatten toward uniform, so their gap compresses. Combined, the gradient scales as 1/T².

If we did not compensate, the soft-target loss would contribute almost nothing to learning at higher temperatures, exactly the opposite of what we want. Multiplying L_soft by T² restores the gradient magnitude to a level comparable to standard cross-entropy. In fact, as T → ∞ the T² KL loss converges to the mean-squared error between the student and teacher logits, which is the classical regression-on-logits view of distillation.

At T=1, a small change in a student's logit produces a gradient of magnitude ~1. At T=3, the same change produces a gradient of magnitude ~1/9, because the logit was divided by 3 and the derivative scales quadratically. At T=5, the gradient drops to ~1/25.

If we do not compensate for this, the soft-target loss contribution would become negligibly small at high temperatures, exactly the opposite of what we want. By multiplying the soft loss by T², we restore the gradients to their natural magnitude. This ensures that the teacher's dark knowledge has a meaningful impact on the student's learning, regardless of which temperature we choose.

This is why the complete distillation loss includes the T² factor:

$$ L_{soft} = T^2 KL(\text{softmax}(z_t / T) || \text{softmax}(z_s / T)) $$

Without T², the soft loss at T=3 would be 9 times weaker than at T=1. With T², all temperatures contribute equally strong gradients, and the only difference is the *quality* of the information (how much dark knowledge is revealed).

### 8.3.5 The temperature Goldilocks zone

As our numerical example demonstrates, temperature is not a "more is better" parameter. There is a Goldilocks zone, typically between T=2 and T=5, where the distribution is soft enough to reveal dark knowledge but still preserves meaningful signal.

Figure 8.11 illustrates this trade-off.

![Figure 8.11 The temperature Goldilocks zone. At T=1, the dark knowledge is hidden, between T=2 and T=5 it is optimally revealed, and beyond T=10, the signal is lost. Smaller student models typically benefit from lower temperatures within this range.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image013.png)
*Figure 8.11 The temperature Goldilocks zone. At T=1, the dark knowledge is hidden, between T=2 and T=5 it is optimally revealed, and beyond T=10, the signal is lost. Smaller student models typically benefit from lower temperatures within this range.*

Why does the optimal temperature depend on the student's size? The answer lies in **capacity**. A very soft distribution (high T) contains a lot of information, subtle relationships between many classes, fine-grained similarity rankings, and small differences in probability. A large student model with many parameters can absorb all of this information and put it to use. A small student model cannot. It lacks the parameters to represent all these subtle relationships, and trying to learn from an overly soft distribution can actually hurt its performance by overwhelming it with information it cannot store.

In practice: 
- **Very small students (1B-3B):** T=2 or T=3 works best. The model has limited capacity and benefits from a clearer, more focused signal. 
- **Medium students (7B-14B):** T=3 or T=4 is typically optimal. Enough capacity to absorb moderate dark knowledge. 
- **Large students (32B-70B):** T=4 or T=5 can be beneficial. The model has enough capacity to leverage the richer information from softer distributions.

Now that we understand temperature and dark knowledge, let's see how to put them into a training objective that actually teaches the student.

##### Listing 8.1 Visualizing temperature-scaled softmax.
```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def visualize_temperature(logits, temps):
    """Show how temperature affects softmax."""
    for T in temps:
        probs = F.softmax(logits / T, dim=-1)
        print(f"T={T:2d}: {probs.tolist()}")

logits = torch.tensor([5.0, 3.0, 1.0, -1.0])
```

## 8.4 Building the distillation loss: From naive to powerful

We now understand that soft targets at a high temperature carry rich dark knowledge. But how do we actually use them to train a student model? In this section, we will walk through three progressive approaches. Each improving on the last, to build the complete knowledge distillation loss function. This mirrors the attempt scaffolding pattern we used for positional encodings in chapter 3.

### 8.4.1 Attempt #1: Training from scratch with hard labels

The simplest approach is to ignore the teacher entirely and train the student model directly on the ground-truth hard labels, exactly as we would train any model from scratch. The student sees each training example, makes a prediction, and receives a gradient based on how far its prediction was from the correct one-hot label.

As illustrated in figure 8.12, this approach provides the student with approximately 1 bit of information per training sample. "The answer is cat" or "the answer is not cat". The student has no way to learn that dogs are more similar to cats than birds are, or that certain incorrect answers are "almost right". It must discover all inter-class relationships from scratch through thousands of training examples.

![Figure 8.12 Attempt #1: Training the student from scratch on hard labels. Each sample provides approximately 1 bit of useful information, and the student has no way to learn inter-class relationships.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image014.png)
*Figure 8.12 Attempt #1: Training the student from scratch on hard labels. Each sample provides approximately 1 bit of useful information, and the student has no way to learn inter-class relationships.*

The result? The student learns, but slowly and imperfectly. Without the teacher's guidance, it must independently discover all of the patterns and relationships that the teacher has already learned. For a small model with limited capacity, this is an uphill battle.

To make this concrete: imagine a student model trained on 10,000 images of cats, dogs, foxes, and birds. Each image provides exactly one bit of information. "This is a cat" or "This is a dog". To learn that cats and dogs are more visually similar to each other than either is to a bird, the student must accumulate statistical evidence over many thousands of examples. It will eventually learn this from the data distribution, but it is a slow, indirect process.

Now imagine the same student trained with soft targets from a teacher. Every single training example simultaneously teaches: (1) the correct label, (2) how similar each wrong answer is to the correct one, and (3) the overall structure of the class space. What took thousands of examples with hard labels can now be learned in a fraction of the time.

### 8.4.2 Attempt #2: Matching the teacher’s output at T=1

A natural next step is to use the teacher's output probabilities as training targets instead of (or in addition to) the hard labels. After all, the teacher's output contains more information than the one-hot label.

But there is a problem: at T=1, the teacher's softmax output is almost as peaked as a hard label. As figure 8.13 shows, when the teacher outputs [0.865, 0.117, 0.016, 0.002], this is barely more informative than the hard label [1, 0, 0, 0].

![Figure 8.13 Attempt #2: Using the teacher’s soft output probabilities at T=1 as training targets. The distribution is still too peaked. The dark knowledge remains hidden in near-zero values, providing only marginal improvement over hard labels.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image015.png)
*Figure 8.13 Attempt #2: Using the teacher’s soft output probabilities at T=1 as training targets. The distribution is still too peaked. The dark knowledge remains hidden in near-zero values, providing only marginal improvement over hard labels.*

The gradients from the near-zero probabilities (0.016 for "fox", 0.002 for "bird") are so small that they have almost no effect on the student's learning. The rich inter-class structure that we know the teacher has learned is effectively invisible at this temperature.

This attempt is slightly better than training from scratch, but the improvement is disappointingly small. We can see the teacher's confidence, but not its nuanced understanding of class relationships.

To understand why, let's think about the gradients. When the student tries to learn from the teacher's T=1 distribution [0.865, 0.117, 0.016, 0.002], the gradient contribution from the "fox" class (probability 0.016) is roughly 50 times smaller than the gradient from the "cat" class (probability 0.865). The gradient from the "bird" class (0.002) is 400 times smaller. These tiny gradients are effectively noise. They contribute almost nothing to the student's learning. The rich inter-class structure that the teacher has learned is present in these tiny numbers, but the gradients are too small to have any effect on the students' weights.

This is the fundamental problem that temperature solves. By raising T, we amplify the relative differences between the small probabilities, bringing them up to a magnitude where their gradients can meaningfully influence the student's learning.

### 8.4.3 Temperature-scaled soft targets

The solution, as we established in section 8.3, is to raise the temperature. By applying softmax at T=3 (or another value in the Goldilocks zone), we transform the teacher's peaked distribution into a rich, informative one where the dark knowledge is clearly visible.

What changes at T=3? Let's look at the gradient magnitudes. The "fox" class, which had a probability of 0.016 at T=1, now has a probability of 0.138, nearly 10 times larger. The "bird" class rises from 0.002 to 0.071. A 35-fold increase. These amplified probabilities produce proportionally larger gradients, which means the student can now *actually learn* from the inter-class relationships that were previously buried in the noise.

Crucially, the *ranking* of the probabilities is preserved: cat > dog > fox > bird. Temperature does not change which class is most likely. It only changes how much probability mass is distributed among the alternatives. The student still learns that "cat" is the correct answer, but now it also learns the full similarity hierarchy.

As figure 8.14 shows, the effect is profound. At T=3, the teacher's soft targets [0.523, 0.268, 0.138, 0.071] provide a wealth of inter-class information in every single training sample. The combined-loss formula visible inside the figure (L = α L_hard + β L_soft T²) is the full knowledge distillation objective; we will derive it in 8.4.4.

![Figure 8.14 The breakthrough: applying temperature T=3 to the softmax unlocks the dark knowledge. The teacher’s probability distribution now reveals rich inter-class relationships, dramatically improving the students’ learning.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image016.png)
*Figure 8.14 The breakthrough: applying temperature T=3 to the softmax unlocks the dark knowledge. The teacher’s probability distribution now reveals rich inter-class relationships, dramatically improving the students’ learning.*

But we cannot simply replace the hard labels with soft targets and call it done. We need both. The hard labels ensure the student still learns the correct answers (grounding), while the soft targets transfer the teacher's dark knowledge (rich inter-class structure). The complete knowledge distillation loss combines these two objectives.

### 8.4.4 The complete distillation loss

The final distillation loss function, as illustrated in figure 8.15, has two components:

1. **The hard-label loss** (L_hard): Standard cross-entropy between the student's predictions (at T=1) and the ground-truth labels. This keeps the student grounded in correctness.
2. **The soft-target loss** (L_soft): KL divergence between the teacher's soft targets (at temperature T) and the student's soft targets (at the same temperature T). This transfers the dark knowledge. The loss is multiplied by T² to compensate for the reduced gradient magnitude at higher temperatures.

The total loss is a weighted combination:

$$ L = \alpha L_{hard} + \beta T^2 KL(\text{teacher\_soft} || \text{student\_soft}) $$

![Figure 8.15 The complete knowledge distillation loss function. Two parallel paths, hard-label cross-entropy (grounding) and soft-target KL divergence (knowledge transfer), combine into a single weighted objective.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image017.png)
*Figure 8.15 The complete knowledge distillation loss function. Two parallel paths, hard-label cross-entropy (grounding) and soft-target KL divergence (knowledge transfer), combine into a single weighted objective.*

> **Definition**  
> What is the distillation loss? The distillation loss is a training objective that combines two terms: a hard-label cross-entropy loss that ensures the student learns correct answers, and a soft-target KL divergence loss (scaled by T²) that transfers the teacher's dark knowledge about inter-class relationships.

The T² scaling factor is a subtle but important detail. When we divide logits by a large T, the resulting gradients are scaled down by a factor of 1/T². To keep the magnitude of the soft-target gradients comparable to the hard-label gradients, we multiply the soft loss by T². This ensures that different temperature values produce comparable training dynamics.

The weighting between the two losses (α and β) is a hyperparameter that controls how much the student relies on ground truth versus the teacher's guidance. In practice, the soft-target loss typically receives more weight than the hard-label loss (β > α), because the dark knowledge from the teacher is the primary source of the student's advantage over training from scratch.

A common configuration is α=0.3 (30% weight on hard labels) and β=0.7 (70% weight on soft targets). This tells the student: "Mostly learn from the teacher, but keep yourself grounded in the actual correct answers." If the teacher has errors or biases, the hard-label component acts as a safety net, preventing the student from blindly inheriting those mistakes.

It is worth pausing to appreciate how elegant this combined objective is. With a single loss function and two hyperparameters (T and α), we can control:

1. **How much dark knowledge is revealed** (via T): higher T reveals more inter-class relationships.
2. **How much the student trusts the teacher** (via α): lower α means more reliance on the teacher.
3. **How strong the gradient signal is** (via T²): automatically scaled to match.

This simplicity is part of why knowledge distillation has become so widely adopted. It requires minimal changes to a standard training pipeline. You add the teacher's forward pass, compute an extra loss term, and adjust two hyperparameters. Everything else (optimizer, learning rate schedule, data loading) stays the same.

##### Listing 8.2 The knowledge distillation loss function.
```python
import torch.nn.functional as F

def distillation_loss(student_logits, teacher_logits, labels, temperature=3.0, alpha=0.3):
    soft_teacher = F.softmax(teacher_logits / temperature, dim=-1)
    soft_student = F.log_softmax(student_logits / temperature, dim=-1)
    kl_loss = F.kl_div(soft_student, soft_teacher, reduction='batchmean')

    hard_loss = F.cross_entropy(student_logits, labels)
    
    return (alpha * hard_loss + (1 - alpha) * temperature**2 * kl_loss)
```

This loss function is the mathematical heart of classical knowledge distillation. The student's parameters are updated to simultaneously minimize both the hard-label error and the distributional mismatch with the teacher.

When we compute this total loss and trigger backpropagation, the gradients are calculated only for the student model. The teacher model is entirely frozen. It acts as a stationary compass, pointing the way, while the student's parameters do all the moving. By backpropagating this combined loss exclusively through the student, we force its small parameter space to organize itself into a compressed approximation of the teacher's deep understanding.

## 8.5 DeepSeek-R1’s distillation recipe

Classical knowledge distillation, as we have just described it, works beautifully for classification tasks where the teacher and student share the same output space. A fixed set of classes with a probability distribution over each. But language models are a different beast entirely. An LLM does not classify images into "cat" or "dog". It generates text, token by token, producing long chains of reasoning that can span thousands of tokens.

This raises a fundamental question: how do you distill *reasoning*?

### 8.5.1 From logits to language: a paradigm shift

In classical distillation, the teacher's value lies in its **logit distribution**. The probabilities it assigns to each class, especially the relative probabilities of incorrect classes (the dark knowledge). The student learns by matching this distribution at a high temperature.

But for a reasoning model like DeepSeek-R1, the teacher's real value is not in its per-token probability distribution. It is in the **text it generates**, specifically, the long chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning traces that show step-by-step problem solving.

Let's think about what this means concretely. When DeepSeek-R1 solves a math problem like "What is the sum of the first 100 prime numbers?", it does not simply output the answer "24,133." It generates a detailed reasoning trace, potentially thousands of tokens long, that might look like:

> *“Let me think about this step by step. First, I need to identify the first 100 prime numbers. The primes start with 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13… I know that the 100th prime is 541. To compute the sum, I can use the fact that… [long chain of reasoning] … Therefore, the sum of the first 100 primes is 24,133.”*

The value of this output is not in the per-token probabilities (should the next token be 'Let' with probability 0.83 or 'I' with probability 0.12?). The value is in the **coherent reasoning strategy**: the decision to break the problem into sub-problems, the mathematical techniques applied at each step, and the verification of the answer. This is what makes DeepSeek-R1 powerful, and this is what needs to be transferred to the student.

This insight led DeepSeek to take a fundamentally different approach to distillation: instead of matching the teacher's logit distribution with KL divergence and temperature scaling, they simply **fine-tune the student on the teacher's generated text**. The teacher generates a complete reasoning trace, and the student learns to produce similar traces through standard supervised fine-tuning. The same next-token prediction objective used in any language model training.

> **Definition**  
> What is chain-of-thought distillation? Chain-of-thought distillation is a technique where a student model is fine-tuned on reasoning traces generated by a teacher model, learning to reproduce the teacher's step-by-step problem-solving approach through standard next-token prediction rather than logit-matching.

### 8.5.2 The 800K training dataset: Rejection sampling at scale

The quality of the training data is everything in this approach. DeepSeek did not simply generate one response per prompt and use it. They used a technique called **rejection sampling** to curate a dataset of exceptionally high quality.

> **Definition**  
> What is rejection sampling? Rejection sampling is a data curation technique where multiple candidate responses are generated for each prompt, each is verified for correctness, and only the correct, well-formatted responses are kept. It is the quality control mechanism behind DeepSeek's distillation dataset.

The process works as shown in figure 8.16: for each prompt, the DeepSeek-R1 teacher generates multiple candidate responses. Each response is then checked for correctness. For math problems, the final answer is compared to the ground truth; for code, the solution is executed and tested. Responses that are correct but poorly formatted (mixed languages, chaotic formatting) are also filtered out. Only clean, correct responses survive.

![Figure 8.16 The rejection sampling process for curating DeepSeek-R1’s distillation dataset. Multiple responses are generated per prompt, verified for correctness, filtered for quality, and only clean, correct answers are kept.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image018.png)
*Figure 8.16 The rejection sampling process for curating DeepSeek-R1’s distillation dataset. Multiple responses are generated per prompt, verified for correctness, filtered for quality, and only clean, correct answers are kept.*

For math and code prompts, verification is relatively straightforward. The answer can be compared to a known ground truth, or the code can be executed to check if it produces the correct output. But what about domains where there is no single correct answer, like STEM reasoning or logical puzzles? For these, DeepSeek used DeepSeek-V3 as a **generative reward model**: the V3 model was given both the ground-truth answer and the candidate response and asked to judge whether the response was correct.

Quality filtering went beyond mere correctness. Responses that were technically correct but 'chaotic and difficult to read', exhibiting mixed languages, excessive code blocks, or incoherent formatting, were also filtered out. This quality control is crucial because the student will learn to mimic the *style* of the training data, not just its content. If the training data contains poorly formatted responses, the student will learn to produce poorly formatted responses.

Through this meticulous process, DeepSeek curated a dataset of approximately 804,745 training samples. Figure 8.18 shows the breakdown by domain.

![Figure 8.18 The composition of DeepSeek-R1’s 800K distillation dataset by domain. Math dominates at 395K samples (49%), followed by Code at 211K (26%), with General, STEM, and Logic making up the remainder.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image020.png)
*Figure 8.18 The composition of DeepSeek-R1’s 800K distillation dataset by domain. Math dominates at 395K samples (49%), followed by Code at 211K (26%), with General, STEM, and Logic making up the remainder.*

The dataset is heavily weighted toward reasoning tasks: math (395K samples) and code (211K samples) alone account for 75% of the total. STEM and logic problems, while smaller in number (about 10K each), are still critical for ensuring the distilled models can reason across scientific and logical domains.

The non-reasoning portion (178K samples of general-purpose content, writing, factual QA, self-cognition, and translation) serves an important role: it ensures the distilled models do not become narrow specialists. Without this general data, a model distilled purely on math and code reasoning might lose its ability to hold a normal conversation, answer factual questions, or follow instructions, capabilities that are essential for a useful AI assistant.

The average sample length of 5,355 tokens is noteworthy. These are not short answers. They are detailed, multi-step reasoning traces that can span thousands of tokens. A typical math solution might include the problem statement, several false starts, corrections, intermediate calculations, and a final verified answer. This rich, verbose format is what allows the student to learn not just *what* answer to give, but *how to think through* the problem step by step.

For the non-reasoning data, DeepSeek used their V3 model to generate responses, sometimes adding chain-of-thought reasoning even for straightforward queries. For very simple queries like 'hello', no chain-of-thought was added. This careful curation ensures that each training sample is appropriate for its domain, reasoning tasks get long, detailed traces, while simple tasks get concise, direct responses.

### 8.5.3 Classical KD versus DeepSeek’s approach

The difference between classical knowledge distillation and DeepSeek's approach is fundamental, and figure 8.19 lays it out side by side.

![Figure 8.19 Two paradigms of knowledge distillation. Classical KD (left) transfers knowledge through logit-matching with KL divergence and temperature scaling. DeepSeek’s approach (right) transfers knowledge through supervised fine-tuning on the teacher’s generated chain-of-thought text.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image021.png)
*Figure 8.19 Two paradigms of knowledge distillation. Classical KD (left) transfers knowledge through logit-matching with KL divergence and temperature scaling. DeepSeek’s approach (right) transfers knowledge through supervised fine-tuning on the teacher’s generated chain-of-thought text.*

In classical KD, the teacher and student both process the same input, and the student learns to match the teacher's output probability distribution using the KL divergence at a high temperature. This requires white-box access to the teacher's logits and involves the temperature scaling and T² correction we discussed earlier.

In DeepSeek's approach, the teacher generates complete text responses (chain-of-thought reasoning traces); these responses are curated through rejection sampling, and the student is fine-tuned on this curated dataset using standard next-token prediction. The same cross-entropy loss is used in any language model training. There is no temperature scaling, no KL divergence, and no need for simultaneous access to both models. The teacher is used only for data generation, and the student never sees the teacher's internal representations.

There is an important subtlety here that is easy to miss. In classical KD, both the teacher and student must be running simultaneously during training. The teacher processes each batch in real-time to produce soft targets. This means the GPU must hold both models in memory at once, which can be expensive when the teacher is large.

In DeepSeek's approach, the teacher is used *only for data generation*, which happens offline before training begins. Once the 800K dataset is curated, the teacher is never needed again. The student trains on a static dataset using standard SFT. There is no need to load the 671B teacher model during student training. This is a massive practical advantage: the data generation step (using the teacher) and the training step (training the student) are completely decoupled.

The DeepSeek-R1 paper is explicit about this design choice: "For distilled models, we apply only SFT and do not include an RL stage, even though incorporating RL could substantially boost model performance." They deliberately kept the distillation process simple to demonstrate its standalone effectiveness. The fact that SFT alone, without any RL, produces models that outperform o1-mini is a testament to how powerful good training data can be.

### 8.5.5 The economics of distillation

Perhaps the most compelling aspect of DeepSeek's approach is its cost-effectiveness. The entire data generation process, running the teacher model to produce 800K high-quality responses through rejection sampling, cost approximately **$10,000** in compute (5,000 H800 GPU hours).

To put this in perspective:

* Training DeepSeek-R1-Zero from scratch with pure RL cost **$202,000** (101K GPU hours).
* Training the full DeepSeek-R1 pipeline cost **$82,000** (41K GPU hours).
* Generating the distillation data cost **$10,000** (5K GPU hours) -roughly **20 times cheaper** than the teacher's own RL training.

And this cost is a one-time investment. Once the 800K dataset exists, any number of student models can be trained on it for a relatively small additional cost (standard SFT on a dense model for 2-3 epochs). DeepSeek trained six models on the same data. A researcher could train dozens more.

This is one of the most powerful properties of distillation: the teacher pays the cost of learning once, and the knowledge can be transferred to an unlimited number of students. The economics strongly favor distillation over training each small model independently with RL.

```python
def rejection_sample(teacher, prompts, n_samples=4):
    dataset = []
    for prompt in prompts:
        candidates = []
        for _ in range(n_samples):
            response = teacher.generate(
                prompt, temperature=0.6,
                top_p=0.95,
                max_tokens=32768)
            candidates.append(response)
        
        for resp in candidates:
            answer = extract_answer(resp)
            truth = get_ground_truth(prompt)
            
            if verify(answer, truth):
                if is_clean(resp):
                    dataset.append((prompt, resp))
                    break
    return dataset
```

## 8.6 Implementing knowledge distillation in PyTorch

We have now covered the theory in detail, from temperature scaling to dark knowledge to DeepSeek's rejection sampling approach. It is time to implement what we have learned. In this section, we will build a complete, runnable knowledge distillation training pipeline in PyTorch.

Following the book's pattern, we will use toy dimensions that are small enough to trace by hand, but that capture the essential structure of the real system. Just as we used 4 tokens and 8-dimensional embeddings in our attention and MoE implementations, our distillation code will use small models with clear, traceable computations. The principles are the same whether the models have 26,000 parameters or 671 billion; only the scale differs.

We will build four things: (1) a teacher model, (2) a student model, (3) the distillation loss function, and (4) the complete training loop that ties them together. By the end of this section, you will have a fully functional knowledge distillation pipeline that you can extend to larger models and real datasets.

### 8.6.1 Defining teacher and student models

For our implementation, we will use the same toy-dimension approach we have followed throughout this book. Our models will be small enough to reason about by hand, but they will capture all the essential structural elements of real-world knowledge distillation.

Our teacher is a 4-layer network with 128-dimensional hidden layers, containing roughly 26,000 parameters. Our student is a 2-layer network with 32-dimensional hidden layers, containing roughly 8,800 parameters, about 3 times smaller. This mirrors the real-world scenario where the teacher is significantly larger and more capable than the student.

Both models share the same embedding layer (100 vocabulary tokens mapped to 64 dimensions) and the same output space (10 classes). The key difference is the depth and width of the hidden layers. The teacher has four layers of 128 neurons, giving it substantially more representational capacity, while the student has just two layers of 32 neurons. The student must learn to approximate the teacher's behavior using a fraction of the parameters.

> **Note**  
> In a real system like DeepSeek, the teacher has 671 billion parameters, and the student ranges from 1.5 billion to 70 billion. Our toy models use the same architectural principles at a much smaller scale. The concepts are identical.

### 8.6.2 The training loop

The distillation training loop looks superficially similar to a standard training loop, but with a crucial structural difference: two models process the same input, but only one of them learns.

Figure 8.22 shows the flow of one training step.

![Figure 8.22 One training step in knowledge distillation. The frozen teacher and the trainable student both process the input. Two loss terms are computed and combined, and only the student’s weights are updated.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image024.png)
*Figure 8.22 One training step in knowledge distillation. The frozen teacher and the trainable student both process the input. Two loss terms are computed and combined, and only the student’s weights are updated.*

Let's walk through this step by step:

1. **Input batch:** A batch of training data (inputs and labels) enters the system.
2. **Teacher forward pass:** The input is fed through the frozen teacher model. We use `torch.no_grad()` to prevent PyTorch from tracking gradients for this pass. The teacher's parameters will never be updated, so computing gradients for it would waste memory and computation.
3. **Student forward pass:** The same input is fed through the student model. Gradients *are* tracked here because we need them to update the student.
4. **Hard-label loss:** The student's raw logits (at T=1) are compared to the ground-truth labels using cross-entropy. This produces L_hard.
5. **Soft-target loss:** Both the teacher's and student's logits are divided by T, passed through softmax, and compared using KL divergence. The result is multiplied by T² to compensate for gradient scaling. This produces L_soft.
6. **Combined loss:** L = α L_hard + (1-α) T² L_soft
7. **Backpropagation:** The combined loss is backpropagated, computing gradients for the student only.
8. **Optimizer step:** The student's parameters are updated.

Figure 8.23 shows this computation with actual numbers, tracing a concrete example from logits through to the final loss value.

![Figure 8.23 Step-by-step computation of the combined distillation loss with actual numbers, showing how teacher and student logits flow through softmax at T=3, KL divergence, T² scaling, and the final weighted combination.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image025.png)
*Figure 8.23 Step-by-step computation of the combined distillation loss with actual numbers, showing how teacher and student logits flow through softmax at T=3, KL divergence, T² scaling, and the final weighted combination.*

Let's trace through this numerical example. Suppose the teacher produces logits [5.0, 3.0, 1.0, -1.0] and the student produces [4.0, 2.5, 1.5, -0.5]. Applying softmax(z / T) at T=3 gives the teacher's soft targets [0.523, 0.268, 0.138, 0.071] and the student's soft targets [0.442, 0.268, 0.192, 0.099]. The KL divergence between these distributions is approximately 0.0199. Multiplied by T² = 9, the soft loss is L_soft ≈ 0.179. The hard-label cross-entropy, computed against the student's standard softmax [0.760, 0.170, 0.062, 0.008] with the true class at index 0, is L_hard ≈ 0.275. With α = 0.3, the combined loss is 0.3 × 0.275 + 0.7 × 0.179 ≈ 0.208

The key insight here is that the soft loss (0.056) is smaller than the hard loss (0.126) in absolute terms, but it carries richer gradient information that steers the student toward the teacher's learned representation of class relationships.

Now let's put this all together in code.

##### Listing 8.4 Complete knowledge distillation training loop.
```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

class TeacherModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab=100, d=64, classes=10):
        super().__init__()
        self.embed = nn.Embedding(vocab, d)
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d, 128), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(128, 128), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(128, 64), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(64, classes)
        )
        
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(self.embed(x).mean(dim=1))

class StudentModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab=100, d=64, classes=10):
        super().__init__()
        self.embed = nn.Embedding(vocab, d)
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d, 32), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(32, classes)
        )
        
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(self.embed(x).mean(dim=1))

def train_distill(teacher, student, loader, epochs=10, T=3.0, alpha=0.3, lr=1e-3):
    teacher.eval()
    opt = torch.optim.Adam(student.parameters(), lr=lr)
    
    for epoch in range(epochs):
        total = 0
        for x, y in loader:
            opt.zero_grad()
            
            with torch.no_grad():
                t_logits = teacher(x)
                
            s_logits = student(x)
            loss = distillation_loss(
                s_logits, t_logits, y,
                temperature=T,
                alpha=alpha
            )
            
            loss.backward()
            opt.step()
            total += loss.item()
            
        print(f"Epoch {epoch+1}: {total/len(loader):.4f}")
```

### 8.6.3 Comparing the three approaches

To see distillation in action, we can train the same student architecture three different ways and compare the results:

1. **Baseline (hard labels only):** The student trains on ground-truth labels with no teacher guidance. This is the Attempt #1 approach from section 8.4.
2. **Distillation (soft targets + hard labels):** The student trains with the full distillation loss from Listing 8.2, using the teacher's soft targets at T=3 along with hard labels.
3. **Teacher (upper bound):** The teacher's own performance, representing the ceiling that the student is trying to approach.

The results consistently show the same pattern: the distilled student significantly outperforms the baseline student, though it does not fully match the teacher. This is the practical proof that dark knowledge transfers through soft targets and improves the student's ability to generalize to unseen data.

The improvement is particularly pronounced on examples where the classes are ambiguous or closely related, exactly the situations where dark knowledge provides the most value. On easy, unambiguous examples (where the teacher's distribution is peaked even at high T), the advantage of distillation is smaller. On hard, borderline examples (where the teacher expresses genuine uncertainty about multiple classes), the advantage is dramatic.

This pattern mirrors what we see at scale in the DeepSeek results: distillation's advantage is most visible on challenging benchmarks like AIME (competition math), where nuanced reasoning is required, and less visible on easier benchmarks where even a scratch-trained model can perform well.

One used large-scale RL to discover reasoning patterns from scratch (over 10,000 policy gradient steps). The other was simply fine-tuned for 2-3 epochs on the teacher's reasoning traces. The student that copied from the teacher decisively outperformed the student that tried to figure things out on its own.

Also, notice what the two RL runs (QwQ-32B and DS-R1-Zero-32B) have in common: they land in almost the same place across every benchmark, despite being trained independently with different RL setups. This convergence is the real signal. It means the plateau is a property of the base architecture at this scale, not of any one team's RL recipe. Distillation is the only path that breaks through that ceiling — and it does so without any RL at all, just by imitating a stronger teacher's reasoning traces. The lesson is not that DeepSeek's RL is weak; it is that 32B parameters of capacity is the binding constraint for RL-from-scratch on this task, and distillation routes around it.

The DeepSeek paper draws two key conclusions from this result:

1. "Distilling more powerful models into smaller ones yields excellent results, whereas smaller models relying on the large-scale RL mentioned in this paper require enormous computational power and may not even achieve the performance of distillation."
2. "While distillation strategies are both economical and effective, advancing beyond the boundaries of intelligence may still require more powerful base models and larger-scale reinforcement learning."

The first conclusion tells us that distillation is the practical path for creating capable small models. The second, more nuanced conclusion tells us that distillation has limits. It can transfer existing knowledge but cannot create new knowledge or push beyond the teacher's capabilities.

Two patterns emerge clearly:

1. **Smooth scaling:** Performance improves steadily as model size increases, from 28.9% at 1.5B to 72.6% at 32B. This is encouraging. It suggests that scaling laws apply to distillation just as they do to other training paradigms.
2. **Base model matters:** The Qwen2.5-Math bases consistently outperform the Llama bases at comparable sizes (Qwen-7B at 55.5% vs Llama-8B at 50.4%). This tells us that distillation is not purely a matter of training. The base model's pre-existing knowledge and architecture interact with the distillation process.

An interesting anomaly: the Llama-70B model (70.0%) actually scores slightly lower than the Qwen-32B model (72.6%), despite being more than twice as large. This suggests that Qwen2.5's general pretraining is stronger at math than Llama-3's general pretraining at this scale, even though both 14B and 32B Qwen variants use Qwen2.5's general base (not the math-specialized one), the mathematical content in Qwen2.5's pretraining corpus appears to give it a persistent edge that more Llama parameters cannot close. The choice of base model is not just an implementation detail. It is a strategic decision that significantly impacts the final distilled model's capabilities.

This finding has broader implications for the field. It tells us that distillation is not a "one size fits all" process. The student's pre-existing knowledge (from its base model's pre-training) interacts with the distilled knowledge in important ways. A base model that already has some mathematical ability will absorb mathematical reasoning patterns more effectively than a base model that is starting from a lower baseline. This is analogous to human learning: a student with a strong foundation in algebra will learn calculus faster than one without, even if they receive the same instruction.

The practical takeaway: when choosing a base model for distillation, consider what capabilities the base already has. For math-heavy applications, a math-specialized base like Qwen2.5-Math is worth the effort. For general-purpose applications, a larger general-purpose base like Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct may be more appropriate, even if it scores slightly lower on math benchmarks.

Why did small models fail at direct RL? The paper reveals an important finding: "During the initial phase of our development, we experimented with smaller-scale models, specifically a 7B dense model and a 16B Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model, as the foundational architectures for RL training. However, these configurations consistently failed to yield meaningful improvements when evaluated on the AIME mark...many as response lengths increased, these smaller models tended toward repetition and were unable to effectively leverage long chains of thought to improve reasoning accuracy."

In other words, small models simply lack the capacity to discover reasoning patterns from scratch through RL. They get stuck in loops of repetitive text, unable to sustain the long chains of thought that complex reasoning demands. The fundamental problem is that RL requires the model to *explore*, to try different approaches, sometimes failing, sometimes succeeding, and gradually learning which strategies work. A 7B model simply does not have enough parameters to store the diverse strategies it discovers during exploration while maintaining the base capabilities it needs for language understanding.

Distillation provides a shortcut around this exploration barrier. The large teacher, with its 671B parameters, has enough capacity to explore, fail, recover, and ultimately discover effective reasoning strategies through RL. Once those strategies are discovered and encoded in the teacher's behavior, they can be directly demonstrated to the student through training data. The student does not need to explore. It just needs to imitate. And imitation, it turns out, is much easier than exploration. Even a 1.5B model can learn to imitate the teacher's reasoning traces, even though it could never have discovered those reasoning patterns on its own through RL.

This is perhaps the deepest insight from DeepSeek's work: **exploration requires capacity, but imitation does not.** Distillation converts the hard problem of exploration into the easier problem of imitation, making reasoning accessible to models that are orders of magnitude smaller than the teacher.

## 8.8 The limits of distillation

Knowledge distillation is powerful, but it is not magic. Before we wrap up, let's be honest about what it can and cannot do.

### 8.8.1 The capacity gap

The first limitation is the **capacity gap**. A student model can only absorb as much knowledge as its architecture allows. When the teacher is vastly more capable than the student, some knowledge inevitably gets lost in compression.

> **Definition**  
> What is the capacity gap? The capacity gap refers to the phenomenon where a student model that is too small relative to the teacher struggles to absorb all the teacher's knowledge, leading to uneven performance across different tasks.

The evidence is clear in DeepSeek's own results. Figure 8.28 shows the 1.5B model's performance across different domains.

![Figure 8.28 The capacity gap in action. The 1.5B distilled model excels at mathematical reasoning (83.9% MATH) but struggles with coding (16.9% LiveCodeBench), indicating that its limited capacity cannot absorb all domains equally.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image030.png)
*Figure 8.28 The capacity gap in action. The 1.5B distilled model excels at mathematical reasoning (83.9% MATH) but struggles with coding (16.9% LiveCodeBench), indicating that its limited capacity cannot absorb all domains equally.*

The 1.5B model achieves an impressive 83.9% on MATH-500 (mathematical reasoning) but only 16.9% on LiveCodeBench (coding). Mathematical reasoning, with its relatively constrained patterns and well-defined solution strategies, compresses well into a small model. Complex coding, with its vast space of possible approaches and syntactic requirements, needs more capacity.

The sweet spot appears to be 7B-14B for general-purpose reasoning and 1.5B-7B for domain-specific (especially mathematical) distillation.

Why does math compress better than coding? The answer lies in the nature of the two domains. Mathematical reasoning, despite its difficulty, follows relatively structured patterns: identify the problem type, apply the appropriate technique, compute step by step, and verify the answer. These patterns are regular enough that even a 1.5B model can learn to reproduce them effectively. Coding, by contrast, requires knowledge of syntax, APIs, design patterns, error handling, and the vast diversity of possible programs. The space of valid code is enormously larger than the space of valid mathematical solutions, and compressing this knowledge into a very small model inevitably loses important details.

This suggests a practical guideline for choosing student model sizes:

* **1.5B-3B:** Suitable for narrow, well-defined reasoning domains (competition math, basic logic). Excellent for deployment on mobile devices and edge hardware.
* **7B-14B:** The sweet spot for general-purpose reasoning that includes both math and coding. Runs on a single consumer GPU.
* **32B-70B:** Near-teacher quality across most domains. Requires professional-grade hardware but is still far more deployable than the 671B teacher.

### 8.8.2 The knowledge frontier

The second, and perhaps more fundamental, limitation is that distillation can only transfer knowledge that the teacher already has. It cannot create new knowledge or push beyond the teacher's capabilities.

This might seem obvious, but the implications are profound. Consider what happens when a distilled model encounters a problem that the teacher itself could not solve. The teacher's training data would contain no correct reasoning traces for that type of problem, because the rejection sampling process only keeps *correct* responses. The student, having been trained exclusively on the teacher's successful problem-solving strategies, has no basis for approaching genuinely novel challenges.

Recent research from May 2025 makes this distinction even sharper. A paper titled "Reinforcement Learning vs. Distillation: Understanding Accuracy and Capability in LLM Reasoning" found that teacher distillation improves *capability* only when it introduces new knowledge absent from the base model. Distilling reasoning *patterns* alone (without new knowledge) improves accuracy but behaves similarly to reinforcement learning. It does not add genuinely new capabilities. In other words, if the base model already knows the relevant facts, teaching it the teacher's reasoning *style* is helpful but not transformative.

As figure 8.29 illustrates, there is a clear boundary between what distillation can achieve and what still requires reinforcement learning or more powerful base models.

![Figure 8.29 The knowledge frontier. Distillation efficiently transfers existing knowledge (chain-of-thought patterns, factual knowledge, mathematical strategies), but novel reasoning capabilities and frontier intelligence still require reinforcement learning and more powerful base models.](https://drek4537l1klr.cloudfront.net/dandekar/v-3/Figures/08__image031.png)
*Figure 8.29 The knowledge frontier. Distillation efficiently transfers existing knowledge (chain-of-thought patterns, factual knowledge, mathematical strategies), but novel reasoning capabilities and frontier intelligence still require reinforcement learning and more powerful base models.*

The DeepSeek paper states this explicitly: "Distillation strategies are both economical and effective, yet advancing beyond the boundaries of intelligence may still require more powerful base models and larger-scale reinforcement learning."

This creates a clear division of labor in the AI development pipeline:

* **Reinforcement learning** discovers new capabilities -it pushes the frontier of what models can do. It is the explorer that maps new territory.
* **Distillation** deploys those capabilities -it makes them accessible in practical, efficient models. It is the bridge that brings the discoveries to everyone.

Both are needed. Neither can replace the other. A world with only RL would have powerful but prohibitively expensive models. A world with only distillation would have efficient but stagnant models, forever limited by what their teachers already know. The combination of both is what makes rapid progress in AI possible.

### 8.8.3 The reproducibility gap

There is one more limitation worth mentioning: the practical difficulty of reproducing DeepSeek's results. While DeepSeek open-sourced all six distilled models (allowing anyone to use them), they did not release the 800K training dataset. The exact prompts, the rejection sampling process, and the quality filters remain proprietary.

This matters because, as we discussed in section 8.5, the quality of the training data is everything in SFT-based distillation. Without the exact prompts and quality filters, attempting to reproduce the dataset yields different results. Community efforts like the Open-R1 project have attempted to replicate DeepSeek's data curation process using publicly available prompts and open-source reward models, with mixed success. The models are good, but not quite at DeepSeek's published benchmark numbers.

The training hyperparameters (learning rates, batch size, epochs) were disclosed, but other details like the optimizer, warmup schedule, gradient accumulation, and weight decay were not. This makes exact reproduction challenging, though the broad approach is clear enough that many groups have successfully trained competitive distilled models.

### 8.8.4 Looking ahead

Several open questions remain at the frontier of knowledge distillation research:

* **RL after distillation.** DeepSeek deliberately did not apply RL to the distilled models, noting that "incorporating RL could substantially boost model performance." Community efforts to apply GRPO to the distilled models, especially for domain-specific tasks like finance and table reasoning, have shown promising results.
* **Scaling laws for distillation.** How does distillation's effectiveness scale with teacher size, student size, and dataset size? Research from Apple and Oxford suggests power-law relationships, but with diminishing returns -a teacher beyond a certain size may not benefit a small student.
* **On-policy distillation.** Instead of training the student on static teacher data, what if the student generates its own attempts and the teacher provides feedback on those specific attempts? Traditional distillation is 'off-policy' -the student trains on the teacher's data, which may not reflect the student's own strengths and weaknesses. On-policy distillation, where the student generates responses and the teacher corrects or scores them in real-time, ensures that the training data is always perfectly aligned with what the student needs to learn next. This approach is more expensive (the teacher must run during student training) but may produce superior results, especially for domains where the student's failure modes differ from the teacher's.
* **Multi-teacher distillation.** What if we distill from multiple teachers simultaneously? Different teachers may have different strengths -one might excel at math while another excels at coding. Combining their knowledge through multi-teacher distillation could produce students that are stronger than any single teacher in certain domains. This is an active area of research with promising initial results.
* **Self-distillation.** Can a model distill from itself? By training on its own best outputs (selected through rejection sampling or best-of-N), a model can iteratively improve without an external teacher. This creates an interesting parallel with the RL approach -the model is essentially using its own best attempts as training data, gradually improving over time.

Knowledge distillation is both the most practical and the most democratic technique in the modern AI toolbox. It is the bridge between frontier research conducted with massive compute budgets and real-world deployment on the devices people actually use. The DeepSeek team demonstrated this with extraordinary results: a tiny 1.5B model that outperforms GPT-4o on math, built with a training process that costs less than the electricity bill of the hardware that runs it.

With this chapter, we have completed our journey through all four stages of building DeepSeek from scratch. Starting from the LLaMA base foundation in Stage 1, through the core architecture innovations of MLA and MoE in Stage 2, the advanced training techniques of MTP and FP8 quantization in Stage 3, and finally the post-training pipeline of supervised fine-tuning, reinforcement learning, and now knowledge distillation in Stage 4, we have built a comprehensive understanding of every component that makes DeepSeek one of the most powerful open-source language models in the world.

The key lesson of distillation, and perhaps of this entire book, is that intelligence is not just about scale. With the right techniques, the knowledge learned by a massive 671-billion-parameter model can be compressed, transferred, and deployed in models small enough to fit on a phone. The frontier of AI is pushed by ever-larger models, but the impact of AI is determined by how effectively that frontier knowledge can be brought to everyone.

## 8.9 Summary

* **Knowledge distillation** transfers a large teacher model’s capabilities to a smaller student model by training the student on the teacher’s soft probability distributions rather than hard one-hot labels.
* Temperature-scaled softmax with T > 1 reveals **dark knowledge** -the relative probabilities of incorrect classes that encode inter-class similarity structure invisible in hard labels.
* The classical distillation loss combines two terms: a hard-label cross-entropy loss (grounding) and a soft-target KL divergence loss scaled by T² (knowledge transfer), weighted by coefficients α and β.
* DeepSeek-R1’s distillation uses a fundamentally different approach: **supervised fine-tuning** on 800K chain-of-thought reasoning traces generated by the teacher via **rejection sampling**, with no temperature scaling or KL divergence.
* DeepSeek’s 800K training dataset contains approximately 395K math, 211K code, 10K STEM, 10K logic, and 178K general samples -curated through rejection sampling that retains only verified-correct, well-formatted responses.
* The distilled DeepSeek-R1-Qwen-1.5B model outperforms GPT-4o on AIME 2024 (28.9% vs 9.3%), demonstrating that reasoning capabilities can be compressed by a factor of approximately 450x in parameter count.
* Distillation dramatically outperforms direct reinforcement learning on the same architecture: the distilled 32B model achieves 72.6% on AIME versus 47.0% for the RL-only variant, a gap of **25.6 percentage points**.
* The **capacity gap** limits what very small students can absorb -a 1.5B model excels at math (83.9% MATH) but struggles with coding (16.9% LiveCodeBench).
* Distillation transfers existing knowledge efficiently but cannot create new capabilities -advancing beyond the teacher’s frontier still requires more powerful base models and larger-scale reinforcement learning.
